Chromatic Fantasia and Fugue in D minor, BWV 903

Johann Sebastian Bach (1685-1750), from Germany, was one of the greatest composers and musician of the Baroque era. In the 18th century, Bach focused on keyboard music most of time. He created German styles by using counterpoint, harmonic and motive organisation.

The Chromatic Fantasia and Fugue in D minor was probably composed around 1720s for harpsichord. Now it is often a piano piece. Bach only composed a few Fantasia and Fugue. In these pieces, fantasia is always presented as prelude before fugue. For this piece, some of scholars think the tile is from number of chromatic modulations in fantasy movement, and others think it is because of the subject melody in Fugue. Also, the content is uncertain. In different manuscripts, some details are not same, even no any modern edition can adjust which is right.

As Koravaar said, different from other Bach's work, this piece is an extraordinary piece which is large, sprawling, emotional and unique in characters. In the piece, the fantasia has 3 main sections: the first part is a true prelude, the second is recitative section, and the third mix them together. In addition, some scholars think the arpeggio part before recitative section is a separate section. In the first section, it has highly virtuosic, and the form is similar to toccata. For the second section, in musically, it is a challenge for pianist to show the huge emotional changing in the recitative part. In harmony aspect, Bach used a wide range of key. Although the main cadence is from tonic D minor to dominant then returning back to tonic, Bach still put some tricks in the fantasy. In the first section, Bach composed the melody with continually key changing to create an excited atmosphere. When music goes into the second section, counterpoint suddenly stops, and melody which sounds like soprano come out. Even in this section, melody sound like chromatic imitations and atonal, Bach still put effort on keeping the key to tonic. And finally, in the end, the key returns back to tonic with a D note pedal.

The subject of the fugue is a long subject. Bach added chromatic scale into the subject as an element. At the end of this piece, Bach wrote an unusual scale. He used octave to perform this a series of note. This is not common in the Baroque era, and Bach often avoided to use it, because parallel 8th does not conform with normal harmony progression. But Bach used it for create heavy and serious feeling. Moroney said: Bach usually obey the rules, but always wrote for the ear.

Mozart Piano Sonata no.1 in C major, K.279

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756-1791) was a representative composer of the Classical era. Mozart was demonstrated in the music of his extraordinary talent when he was young. He was not only good at playing violin and piano, but also composed his music when he was 5. His music is enriching of amount of character and emotional, almost including full range of human's emotions. Fischer has said: “Mozart is a touchstone of the heart. If I want to show my love to a dear person, I sit down at the piano and play for her a piece of Mozart”.

Mozart composed his first 6 pieces of sonata in 1775 when he was in Munich. Also, this is the first time Mozart composed for fortepiano. He took full advantage of the obvious feature of fortepiano to compose this sonata. Fortepiano has a larger range of dynamic. So, in these pieces, there are serval dynamic marks appears in the score, and he use bass part to present sonorous sounds.

It is widely believed that Allegro in K.279 is Mozart's first work. All three movements are in sonata form. Different from other sonata, as the first work, Allegro movement does not have a significant character in the beginning. The theme still has a Baroque feeling. All melodies and structure in the first movement are straightforward. But end of this movement, Mozart extended fragmentation in the last 2 bars to make surprise.

The character in the second movement is richer than the first movement. In the beginning, Mozart created warm and peaceful feeling. He always composed the slow movement with an idyllic feeling, and he also used in this movement. In the middle of exposition, Mozart changed dynamic continually in each fragmentation to push the feeling to the negative side. Paavili illustrated that: “Mozart was a master of articulating the nuances of happiness and unhappiness”. In this movement, he used different dynamic to aggravate the conflict of happiness and sadness.

Prokofiev Piano Sonata no.6 in A major, Op.82

Sergei Sergeyevich Prokofiev (1891-1953) was a Russian Soviet composer. His famous works includes the March from The Love for Three Oranges, the ballet Romeo and Juliet and Peter and the Wolf. Because of these works, he is regarded as one of the greatest composers of the 20th century.

The Nazi invasion of the USSR incited him to compose amount of works. Prokofiev Piano Sonata no.6&7&8 are the pieces he created during this time, and these three sonatas are often called “3 War Sonatas”. Prokofiev Piano Sonata no.6 is the first of “War Sonatas”. Prokofiev started to write these pieces from 1939, and he finished composing no.6 in 1940. This sonata includes number of elements of Prokofiev’s piano composing style, such as complex rhythm, percussive attack, and dissonance harmonies. This piece had played twice in 1940. On April 8th, Prokofiev played on a broadcast, and on November 26th, Sviatoslav Richter played it in his public concert at the Small Hall of the Moscow Conservatory. After well- received debuts from the first two performance, many musicians gave positive feedback. Even Dmitri Shostakovich, who’s a picky person, said: “The Sixth Sonata is magnificent, from beginning to end. I am happy that I had the opportunity to hear it two times, and regret that it was only two times.” In the biography of Prokofiev, Hanson noted: “The composer’s joyous, vigorous, intensely musical personality is expressed in every opus and in none more strikingly than the 6th sonata. It was one of the most extroverted sonatas he was to write, and one of the finest.”

Prokofiev Piano Sonata no.6 has 4 movements. Although the content of this piece is modernity, this piece is still a traditional sonata form. Main theme of the first movement is aggressive. 3 notes motive in excited rhythm creates a nervous and vehement atmosphere. In addition, it is impressive that these motive repeats amount of time in the first movement even appears in the 4th movement. From the second subject, melody becomes more tranquillo. In the development section, Prokofiev added motive in it and gradually lead music to recapitulation. In the last bar, Prokofiev used the lowest range of piano to create an imagination of war. In addition, he also used it in the end of last movement. The second movement is a quick march, and the middle section is more cantabile. The quiet ending in the second movement makes emotion move to the third movement more natural. The waltz feeling in lentissimo and luxuriant harmony makes this movement sound expressive and fragile. To the last movement, the character changed and is variable. It is a toccata with different emotions. Sometimes it is breathless, sometimes it has playful dancing feeling. When the motive in the 1st movement return in this movement, whatever it is played by slow tempo or fast, it still creates the atmosphere like horror. In the last 2 bars, Prokofiev used semiquavers play the highest and lowest range of keyboard to push the feeling of war to the highest point.
